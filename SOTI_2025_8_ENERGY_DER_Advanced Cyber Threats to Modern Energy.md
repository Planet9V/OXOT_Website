

# **SOTI\_2025\_8\_ENERGY\_DER\_Advanced Cyber Threats to Modern Energy**

### **Executive Summary**

The global energy sector, a cornerstone of national and economic security, is facing an unprecedented and rapidly evolving cyber threat landscape. This report provides a detailed analysis of the top three threat actors targeting this critical infrastructure, examining their advanced techniques, and focusing on the systemic vulnerabilities present in new distributed energy resources (DERs) and the Internet of Things (IoT). The analysis reveals a complex nexus of geopolitical, financial, and ideological motivations driving these attacks. From sophisticated nation-state actors like Russia's Sandworm and North Korea's Lazarus Group to prolific ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) collectives like RansomHub/DragonForce, adversaries are demonstrating a growing capacity for both data theft and physical disruption.

The convergence of Information Technology (IT), Operational Technology (OT), and the proliferation of interconnected IoT devices has exponentially expanded the attack surface, introducing critical vulnerabilities at the "edge" of the grid. This report examines how these threats exploit flaws in specific communication protocols, embedded software, and unpatched legacy systems. A detailed review of recent, actively exploited vulnerabilities, including CVE-2024-7490 and CVE-2024-6047, highlights the immediate and tangible risks facing the industry. The analysis culminates in a series of strategic recommendations, including the prioritization of patch management for known exploited vulnerabilities, robust network segmentation, and enhanced supply chain security, to build a more resilient and proactive defense posture.

## **1\. The Evolving Threat Landscape: A Nexus of Geopolitics, Profit, and Technology**

The energy sector's increasing digitalization and decentralization have fundamentally reshaped its security paradigm. The motivations of adversaries have diversified, moving beyond simple espionage to a complex blend of profit, state-sponsored intelligence gathering, and hybrid warfare tactics. This shift is not merely an increase in volume but a fundamental change in the nature of the threats themselves.

### **1.1. Shifting Motivations: The Convergence of Ideology, Espionage, and Profit**

Attacks on the energy sector today are driven by a variety of sophisticated actors with distinct, yet sometimes overlapping, objectives. Financially motivated attacks from ransomware groups like RansomHub/DragonForce and HellCat are a pervasive threat, frequently targeting large enterprises for maximum financial disruption.1 These attacks are often high-impact, as demonstrated by RansomHub's August 2024 breach of Halliburton, which resulted in a $35 million loss and forced the company to shut down its IT systems.1

Simultaneously, nation-state actors linked to China, Iran, and North Korea have been increasingly observed targeting the energy sector for espionage and sabotage.1 The objectives of these groups extend beyond financial gain to include intelligence gathering on critical technologies, political influence, and maintaining a foothold for future disruptive operations. The threat landscape is further complicated by the rise of politically motivated hacktivist groups, such as S16 and Noname057(16), who publicize alleged compromises to build credibility and sow discord in alignment with specific geopolitical interests.1

A critical observation is the blurring of lines between these categories. Nation-state groups, most notably North Korea's Lazarus Group, have historically engaged in large-scale financial cybercrime to fund state interests.2 Similarly, some ransomware collectives are believed to have state affiliations, such as the Russia-nexus ransomware group Cl0P.1 This complex overlap means that organizations must prepare for adversaries who possess the advanced resources and long-term objectives of a nation-state while simultaneously being driven by the immediate financial incentive of organized crime.

### **1.2. Convergence and Attack Surface Expansion: From IT to the Grid's Edge**

The energy sector's shift toward smart grids and distributed energy resources (DERs) has created an unprecedented level of interconnectedness between previously isolated systems. The integration of Information Technology (IT), Operational Technology (OT), and the Internet of Things (IoT) has expanded the attack surface exponentially.4 OT networks, which once relied on physical isolation or "air gaps" for security, are now directly connected to IT networks and, by extension, the internet. This convergence enables real-time data processing, predictive maintenance, and dynamic load balancing, but it also introduces a multitude of new access points and dependencies on third-party vendors.5

The complexity of these modern systems makes them highly vulnerable. The supply chain, in particular, has become a significant risk vector. Energy infrastructure increasingly relies on external software providers, cloud services, and imported hardware components. A compromise within any of these third-party systems can provide an attacker with a direct path into critical grid operations.4 This problem is compounded by the widespread use of legacy systems and outdated operational technology, which suffer from inadequate security controls, are difficult to patch, and are often prohibitively expensive to upgrade.5

### **1.3. A Brief History of Energy Sector Attacks: A Blueprint for Cyber Warfare**

To understand the current threat environment, it is essential to contextualize it with a review of historical attacks that have served as a blueprint for modern cyber warfare. The attacks on the Ukrainian power grid in 2015 and 2016, attributed to the Russian APT group Sandworm, are a foundational case study in cyber-physical attacks.7

The 2015 attack, which was the first publicly acknowledged cyberattack to cause a power outage, affected 230,000 consumers for up to six hours.7 The attack was a multi-stage operation. It began with a spear-phishing campaign that delivered BlackEnergy 3 malware to corporate networks.7 After gaining a foothold, the attackers pivoted to the OT network to control switches and disrupt the power supply. The operation concluded with the deployment of KillDisk malware to destroy files on servers and a Denial-of-Service (DoS) attack on the company's call center to delay a response.7 The sophistication of this incident established a new and ominous precedent for critical infrastructure security worldwide.8

The 2016 attack demonstrated a clear progression in adversary capability. This attack utilized a purpose-built, modular malware framework known as Industroyer (or CRASHOVERRIDE).11 Unlike the more general-purpose malware used in 2015, Industroyer was specifically designed to communicate with industrial control system (ICS) protocols such as IEC 60870-5-104 and IEC 61850\.11 This enabled the attackers to directly manipulate protective relays and circuit breakers, causing a power outage for a portion of Kyiv. The use of this highly-tailored malware demonstrates a causal relationship between geopolitical conflict and the development of sophisticated, protocol-specific cyber weapons designed to directly influence the physical world.11 Further analysis has shown that this attack was not a standalone event but coincided with coordinated missile strikes on critical infrastructure, underscoring a strategic shift toward integrating cyber operations with kinetic warfare to maximize disruption and psychological impact.4

Another notable event, the NotPetya attack of 2017, also linked to Russian state-sponsored actors, demonstrated the potential for destructive, widespread, and financially devastating attacks that can spill over globally.14 Originally designed to target Ukrainian businesses, the malware spread worldwide, causing an estimated $10 billion in damage.14 These historical events provide a crucial foundation for understanding the advanced and destructive capabilities of threat actors today.

## **2\. Deep Dive into the Top Three Threat Actors**

This section provides a detailed profile of the three most significant threat actors currently targeting the energy sector, distinguishing their motivations, tactics, and targets.

### **2.1. Threat Actor 1: RansomHub/DragonForce \- The Financially Motivated RaaS Collective**

**Profiling the Group:** RansomHub is a prominent example of a modern, financially-motivated ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) collective. The group's activities are characterized by high-value, high-impact ransomware attacks on critical infrastructure. The most significant recent attack was the breach of Halliburton, a major oil service company, in August 2024\. This attack cost the company $35 million in losses and required them to shut down IT systems and disconnect customers.1 In a development observed in April 2025, RansomHub transitioned to using DragonForce's RaaS infrastructure, illustrating the fluid and evolving nature of these criminal organizations.1 DragonForce also claimed responsibility for a data breach in December 2024 that impacted Ikav Energy, a renewable energy investment firm, resulting in the compromise of sensitive data, including American social security numbers.1

**Advanced TTPs:** These RaaS collectives frequently leverage supply chain vulnerabilities to gain initial access. The Cl0P group, which is believed to be a Russia-nexus ransomware collective, utilized the 2023 MOVEit supply-chain attack to compromise at least 15 energy sector victims.1 The DragonForce breach of Ikav Energy also highlights this strategy, as the attack did not directly target a primary energy firm but one of its partners, demonstrating a sophisticated understanding of network dependencies and third-party risks.1 A key tactic is the use of double-extortion, where attackers not only encrypt data but also steal it and threaten to leak it if a ransom is not paid.16 Emerging RaaS groups like Qilin, which led ransomware activity in July 2025, have even introduced features like a "Call Lawyer" option to increase pressure on victims to pay.16 The commoditization of cybercrime is evident in the RaaS business model, which relies on a sophisticated supply chain that includes initial access brokers who provide the entry points for attacks.1

**Targets and Objectives:** Their primary targets are large enterprises with complex, distributed infrastructure across various critical sectors, including energy, manufacturing, and transportation.16 The ultimate goal is maximum financial disruption and payout, with ransom demands often ranging from hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars.18 These groups are opportunistic and focused on industries that can pay large ransoms to restore functionality, making the energy sector a prime target.5

### **2.2. Threat Actor 2: Lazarus Group \- The Prolific and Strategic Nation-State Espionage Group**

**Profiling the Group:** The Lazarus Group, also known as APT38, is a state-sponsored threat actor from North Korea.3 The group has a long history of conducting sophisticated cyber espionage and financial cybercrime operations to further the strategic and financial interests of the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK).20 Their activities are directed by the Reconnaissance General Bureau (RGB), a North Korean military intelligence agency.20 While they have traditionally focused on financial institutions and cryptocurrency platforms to generate revenue, they have also been observed targeting government contractors and companies involved in key military and energy technologies to gather intelligence.3

**Advanced TTPs:** The group's tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) are characterized by stealth, a long-term focus on intelligence gathering, and a high degree of adaptability. In a 2022 campaign, Lazarus targeted energy providers in the United States, Canada, and Japan by exploiting known vulnerabilities in VMWare Horizon, specifically the Log4j vulnerability, to gain initial access.19 Post-exploitation, they deployed a toolkit of custom Remote Access Trojans (RATs) such as VSingle, YamaBot, and MagicRAT to maintain a persistent presence and exfiltrate data of interest.19

A more recent and novel TTP involves impersonating remote IT workers to gain legitimate access to corporate networks.24 Using AI to create fake personas, resumes, and even forge identification documents, these threat actors apply for IT jobs at targeted companies. Once hired, they use the company's hardware to gain remote access and exfiltrate data, bypassing traditional perimeter defenses.24 This is a highly sophisticated approach that leverages human intelligence and modern AI tools to achieve a strategic objective.

**Targets and Objectives:** The primary objective of the Lazarus Group is to establish long-term access and exfiltrate data that is of strategic value to the North Korean state.22 The group's targeting of the energy sector is focused on intelligence gathering rather than outright destruction, seeking information on key technologies and operational capabilities.22 The financial gains from their cryptocurrency and financial institution attacks are a secondary, but still crucial, objective used to fund their broader state-sponsored operations.2

### **2.3. Threat Actor 3: Sandworm \- The Destructive and Geopolitically Aligned Cyber-Physical Adversary**

**Profiling the Group:** Sandworm is a Russian-linked advanced persistent threat (APT) group known for its highly destructive cyber operations, particularly against Ukrainian critical infrastructure.10 The group is considered one of the most mature offensive OT actors, with a demonstrated capability to cause real-world physical disruption, making it a preeminent threat to global energy security.13

**Advanced TTPs:** Sandworm’s TTPs are the most advanced in the cyber-physical domain, with a clear focus on sabotage. Their operations often begin with initial access to corporate IT networks through methods like spear-phishing, as seen in the 2015 attack.7 However, the most significant characteristic of this group is their use of purpose-built malware to directly interact with ICS. In the 2016 attack, Sandworm utilized Industroyer/CRASHOVERRIDE, a modular malware designed to speak the native languages of ICS protocols like IEC 60870-5-104 and IEC 61850\.11 This enabled them to manipulate protective relays and circuit breakers, causing a power outage. A more recent evolution of their TTPs involves "Living off the Land" (LotL) techniques, where they use native system binaries and trusted tools to execute attacks. This approach reduces the resources required to conduct a cyber-physical attack and makes it more difficult for defenders to detect, as the activity appears to be legitimate.13 The strategic progression from generic malware like BlackEnergy in 2015 to the highly-specialized Industroyer in 2016 demonstrates a clear and deliberate focus on developing tools for targeted, physical sabotage.

**Targets and Objectives:** Sandworm's objectives are geopolitical and destructive. The group targets critical infrastructure with the explicit goal of causing physical damage and widespread disruption, often in coordination with kinetic military strikes.4 Their attacks are designed to cause a significant psychological blow against a country and its population, demonstrating the use of cyber operations as a core component of a broader, multi-domain conflict strategy.4

## **3\. The New Edge: Threat Vectors in Distributed Energy and IoT**

The modern energy grid is defined by its decentralization, with Distributed Energy Resources (DERs) such as solar panels, wind turbines, and microgrids increasingly integrated with the core infrastructure. This shift has placed smart meters and other grid-connected IoT devices at the forefront of the new threat landscape, presenting a host of unique vulnerabilities.

### **3.1. Smart Meters and Grid IoT: The Critical Role of Edge Devices**

Smart meters are a vital component of the smart grid, providing real-time data on energy consumption, enabling dynamic load balancing, and facilitating a more efficient, self-healing grid.6 However, this critical role at the "edge" of the grid also creates a significantly larger number of attack entry points compared to traditional centralized systems.28 These devices often have lower security standards than enterprise systems, rely on third-party embedded software 29, and are difficult to patch and update over their long operational lifecycles.29 This combination of factors makes them prime targets for a variety of attacks, including data manipulation, denial-of-service (DoS) attacks, and recruitment into botnets.27

The vulnerability of these interconnected devices is a systemic issue. A failure to provide long-term patches for legacy devices creates an "IoT bottleneck" that attackers are actively exploiting to build their arsenals. The active exploitation of command injection vulnerabilities in discontinued GeoVision IoT devices (CVE-2024-6047, CVE-2024-11120) to download and execute Mirai-based malware is a direct example of this exploitation.31 These compromised devices can then be weaponized to launch massive distributed DoS (DDoS) attacks against the energy grid's communication networks.9 The ability of an adversary to manipulate real-time data from these devices can lead to a cascade of physical failures, as operators acting on false information could unintentionally overload power lines.9

### **3.2. Communications Layer: Threats and Vulnerabilities**

The integrity of the modern smart grid is fundamentally dependent on its communication infrastructure. The diversity of communication methods, while providing resilience, also introduces a variety of unique threats.

* **Power Line Communication (PLC):** Standards like G3-PLC and PRIME utilize existing electrical infrastructure for data transmission, making them cost-effective and scalable.34 However, the protocols and devices that use them can be vulnerable. A 2013 CISA advisory on PLC devices highlighted risks such as improper access control, buffer overflows, and authentication bypass via replay attacks.35 For instance, a DoS condition could be triggered by sending an unauthorized CIP message to a PLC device, causing a loss of availability and communication.35 The inherent ubiquity of the power lines themselves provides a massive physical attack vector.  
* **Wireless Communication (Cellular, RF, Wi-Fi):** Smart meters and DERs use a variety of wireless technologies, including cellular networks (GPRS, LTE-M), wireless mesh networks, and short-range RF.34 While cellular networks are generally considered scalable and secure, they still require robust encryption and authentication protocols to protect against eavesdropping and data tampering.34 These wireless links are susceptible to man-in-the-middle (MITM) attacks, where an adversary can intercept and modify data packets.33 The SunSpec Modbus protocol, which is often used in smart inverters, can be exploited via data-flooding attacks to degrade the inverter’s functionality.33  
* **Protocol-Specific Vulnerabilities:** The reliance on open and sometimes insecure communication protocols presents a major risk. A recent report on the OpenADR protocol, which is used for DER management, identified over 700 implementation vulnerabilities in its open-source Java library.28 Such flaws can allow remote attackers to access sensitive data, send false control commands, and manipulate market operations.28 This demonstrates that the attack surface extends not just to the hardware but also to the underlying software and protocols that govern grid communication.

## **4\. Vulnerabilities, Exploitation, and the Road to Resilience**

A recent SixMap report on the external attack surfaces of 21 major U.S. energy companies revealed an alarming number of security flaws, highlighting a systemic vulnerability that puts the entire industry at risk.37

### **4.1. The Threat of Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEVs): A Systemic Risk**

The SixMap report found over 5,750 vulnerabilities on the networks of these companies, with two-thirds categorized as high- or critical-severity.37 The most concerning finding is that nearly 380 of these vulnerabilities are actively being exploited in the wild, posing an immediate and direct threat to operations.37 This is not an isolated problem; the report identified 43 unique CVEs that were present in at least 10 of the 21 companies, suggesting a widespread reliance on similar legacy systems and a lack of consistent patch management.37 The lack of a consistent, prioritized patch management process is the root cause of this systemic risk. The difficulty and high cost of updating legacy OT systems is a major contributing factor.5

**Recent and Actively Exploited CVEs:**

* **CVE-2024-7490 (Microchip Advanced Software Framework):** This is a critical buffer overflow vulnerability in the Advanced Software Framework (ASF) DHCP server, a component widely used in IoT devices like sensors and industrial controllers.29 Exploiting this flaw allows remote attackers to execute malicious code on targeted devices, giving them control over entire systems. The vulnerability is particularly dangerous because it stems from the widespread dependence on third-party software, which often lacks rigorous security controls, leaving a complex supply chain exposed.29  
* **CVE-2024-6047 and CVE-2024-11120 (GeoVision IoT Devices):** These command injection vulnerabilities were recently observed being actively exploited to download and execute Mirai-based malware, turning affected devices into botnet components.31 This is a prime example of how adversaries are building massive botnets by targeting poorly secured and unpatched IoT devices, which can then be weaponized to launch DDoS attacks against critical infrastructure communication networks.31  
* **CVE-2025-49704 and CVE-2025-49706 (SharePoint):** Microsoft has observed Chinese state-sponsored actors, including Linen Typhoon, Violet Typhoon, and Storm-2603, actively exploiting these vulnerabilities to gain initial access to on-premises SharePoint servers.39 This highlights how traditional IT vulnerabilities are used as a gateway to enterprise networks, which can then provide a foothold for lateral movement into more critical OT environments.

### **4.2. Vulnerability Discovery and Exploitation Methods**

Threat actors employ a multi-pronged approach to discover and weaponize vulnerabilities. Initial access is often gained through well-established methods like spear-phishing, supply chain compromise, or exploiting publicly known vulnerabilities in internet-facing services.1

However, adversaries are becoming more sophisticated in their reconnaissance efforts. They perform automated scans of an enterprise’s external attack surface, specifically targeting overlooked non-standard ports and IPv6 addresses that traditional security tools often miss.37 This highlights a critical "blind spot" in the industry's defensive posture, as the failure to monitor the entire digital estate creates a perfect opportunity for adversaries to exploit.38 Human intelligence is also a key component, with actors like the Lazarus Group using AI-generated personas to impersonate IT workers and gain internal network access.24

## **5\. Detailed Recommendations and Defensive Strategies**

To counter the escalating threats to the energy sector, organizations must transition from a reactive to a proactive security model. The following strategies are essential for building robust and resilient cyber defenses.

* **Foundational Security Practices:**  
  * **Network Segmentation:** Implement robust network segmentation to divide the smart grid network into isolated segments. This is a crucial measure to limit lateral movement for attackers and contain a potential breach to a single segment, especially for OT and IoT devices.29  
  * **Patch Management:** Prioritize the remediation of known exploited vulnerabilities (KEVs) identified by CISA and other threat intelligence agencies.38 This requires a dedicated and well-resourced patching program, particularly for internet-facing systems and legacy OT equipment.  
  * **Access Control:** Enforce the principle of least privilege and implement strong, unique, and multi-factor authentication (MFA) across all systems, from corporate IT to OT control panels.27  
* **Advanced Threat Detection and Response:**  
  * **Real-time Monitoring:** Deploy advanced threat detection solutions, including AI and machine learning, for real-time anomaly detection in DER sensor data and grid communications.27 This can help identify false data injection attacks before they lead to physical disruption.  
  * **Intrusion Detection and Prevention Systems (IDPS):** Implement IDPS specifically tailored to monitor OT protocols and networks for suspicious behavior.32  
  * **Incident Response:** Develop and regularly test a comprehensive incident response plan, including robust backup and recovery strategies, to minimize the impact of a successful attack.32  
* **Securing the Supply Chain and Third-Party Relationships:**  
  * Given the prevalence of supply chain attacks, energy companies must conduct thorough security assessments of all third-party vendors, from hardware components to software providers.4  
  * This includes ensuring that embedded software, like Microchip's ASF, has rigorous security controls from the outset and that vendors provide a clear and long-term patching roadmap.29  
* **Human and Physical Security:**  
  * **Security Awareness:** The human factor remains a significant vulnerability.24 Regular security awareness training is essential to combat social engineering, phishing, and insider threats.32  
  * **Physical Security:** Maintain robust physical security for critical assets, such as substations and data centers, to prevent unauthorized entry and protect against hybrid threats that combine cyber and physical sabotage.4
